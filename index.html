Research Statement

→ 2025 CV

I am a qualitative researcher working at the intersection of Human Computer Interaction, Science and Technology Studies, and Craft Studies. I explore how human sensing, embodied ways of knowing, and computational systems intermingle in situated practice. My process as a researcher is to carefully listen to participants, to gently observe the day-to-day, to ask questions to test my understanding, and to align myself as best I can with the sensorial experiences of others. I look for sites where I can turn to the social, distributed, and emergent nature of sensing and study how new computational process reconfigure work. Through this work I aim to assist with the development of more responsive, compassionate, and functional technological systems.

Projects

The following projects represent key investigations that informed my dissertation on the sensory aspects of human-machine collaboration. Each explores how embodied knowledge and sensory experience are transformed when new technologies are introduced into established practices. Together, they reveal how sensing is never individual but always socially distributed, technologically mediated, and emergent in practice. These studies help us better understand how to design more responsive systems that recognize and support the rich sensory work that makes collaboration possible.

(Re)collecting Craft: Reviving Materials, Techniques, and Pedagogies of Craft for Computational Makers

We explore craft's modern formation at the dawn of the Industrial Revolution across three formative sites: Scandinavian Slöyd, British Arts and Crafts, and Japanese Mingei. From this review we identify three key (and still evolving) features: craft's accountabilities to natural materials and local ecologies; craft's holistic ways of making with 'head, heart, and hand'; and craft's distinctly collaborative and embodied styles of teaching and learning. Sensing (Co)operations: Articulation and Compensation in the Robotic Operating Room

Drawing on ethnographic fieldwork in two different teaching hospitals that deployed the da Vinci surgical robot, this paper traces how the introduction of robotics reconfigures the sensory environment of surgery and how surgeons and their teams recalibrate their work in response. We illustrate how sensory (re)articulations and compensations allow the surgeon and surgical teams to adapt to a more-than-human sensorium and conclude by advocating new forms of sensory-aware design capable of enhancing and supporting embodied sensory conditions both individually and across teams. Digital Entanglements: Craft, Computation and Collaboration in Fine Art Furniture Production

Drawing on studio visits and interviews with fine art furniture maker Wendell Castle and his team, we investigate one studio's experience with integrating digital fabrication tools into their studio practice, and its implications for the collective organization of work and creativity. We chart new forms of creative practice and material flow that emerge from this encounter, and show how remediations of craft in the Castle studio may pose questions and opportunities for wider CSCW concerns around craft, creativity, and design.
