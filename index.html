Research Statement

I am a qualitative researcher working at the intersection of Human Computer Interaction, Science and Technology Studies, and Craft Studies, and a PhD candidate in Science and Technology Studies at Cornell University.

As a researcher, I am drawn to sites where new computational processes reconfigure work - particularly work that is collaborative, embodied and sensorially-informed. I explore how human sensing, tacit knowledge, and computational systems intermingle in situated practice, curiosities that often center on the evolution of craft-based work (such as new robotic tools being taken up in operating rooms or computational acoustics being taken up by violin makers).

My method is ethnographic. I practice carefully listening to participants, gently observing their day-to-day, asking questions that test my understanding, and attuning myself with the sensorial experiences of others. Through this, I aim to assist with the development of more responsive, heterogenous, compassionate, and functional technological systems.

Projects

How can large language models encompass more situated, embodied, and tacit ways of knowing?

This experimental project (with Bernadette Boscoe and Chandler Campbell of Southern Oregon University) funded by the Sloan Foundation explores how science labs could make use of a rag llm in the everyday to help make scientific work more durable, actionable, shareable, and discoverable. What kinds of knowledge are incorporated into large language models, and what is excluded? Aquillm is a place-based AI "archive" that aims to find out. Ongoing project

How has the violin (and the world that surrounds and encompasses it) been made durable?

And, how does it react and respond to new computational processes and sensing technologies? This project examines the violin as a boundary object that traverses artistic and scientific domains, exploring how traditional craft knowledge intersects with digital measurement and modeling tools. Through ethnographic observations and interviews with luthiers, acousticians, and performers, I investigate how the materiality of instruments, embodied practices of musicians, and computational analysis co-constitute a shared sonic world. Ongoing project.

What can craft movements teach us about human-centered computing?

My historical analysis of craft traditions—Scandinavian Slöyd, British Arts and Crafts, and Japanese Mingei—reveals three enduring principles that can inform more responsive technological design: sensitivity to materials and ecological contexts; integration of cognitive, emotional, and physical dimensions of making; and embodied, collaborative learning environments. How do surgical teams adapt to robot-mediated sensing?

Drawing on ethnographic fieldwork in two teaching hospitals using the da Vinci surgical robot, I examine how robotic mediation transforms the sensory environment of surgery and necessitates new collaborative practices. I show how surgical teams develop sophisticated compensatory techniques and sensory articulations to overcome the limitations of technologically mediated perception. These adaptations create a distributed, more-than-human sensorium that extends beyond individual bodies to encompass the entire surgical team and technological system. How do digital tools transform craft and design practices in fine art furniture making?

Through studio visits and interviews with renowned fine art furniture maker Wendell Castle and his team, I investigate the complex integration of digital fabrication technologies into established craft practice. Our research reveals how this hybrid environment creates new forms of creative expression, reshapes material relationships, and transforms collaborative workflows. Rather than diminishing craft knowledge, we find that digital tools become entangled with embodied expertise, creating distinctive socio-technical arrangements where digital and physical processes mutually inform each other. How can urban permaculture help us develop and tell stories about plant-people relations?

This passion project explores the transformation of a small urban backyard into an experimental permaculture system while also becoming a place to develop new ways of sensing with plants. The garden here and acts as a research site and a material argument for coming to understand our own plant-people relations. This on-going project led to the collaborative creation of a community 'zine meant to probe and collect other stories about plants and people. This collaborative project (with Lisette Lorenz and Martin Abbott) unfolded as a book chapter that pondered the creative ways we might narrate the multispecies world. Read Zine (opens in new window)
