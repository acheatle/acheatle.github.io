Research Statement

→ 2025

I explore how human sensing and computational systems mutually transform established ways of knowing and making, by examining the situated practices through which sensory perception and action are accomplished. By "examining", I mean to carefully listen to those who are doing the work, to gently observe their day-to-day, to ask questions to see if I understand how they navigate through change, to try and "sense" their lived experiences by being with them empathatically in time and place. I look for sites where I can turn to the social, distributed, and emergent nature of sensing, often at the intersections of HCI and embodied work. By doing so I share insights into diverse sensory experiences and developing more responsive, compassionate, and functional technological systems.

Q: When we say craft, what do we mean? A study in craft's origins.

(Re)collecting Craft: Reviving Materials, Techniques, and Pedagogies of Craft for Computational Makers

Drawing from literature in HCI, craft studies and Science and Technology Studies (STS), we explore craft's modern formation at the dawn of the Industrial Revolution across three formative sites: Scandinavian Slöyd, British Arts and Crafts, and Japanese Mingei. From this review we identify three key (and still evolving) features: craft's accountabilities to natural materials and local ecologies; craft's holistic ways of making with 'head, heart, and hand'; and craft's distinctly collaborative and embodied styles of teaching and learning.

Q: How does sensorial and craft-based teamwork adapt to a robotic partner? Findings from the robotic operating room.

Sensing Co-operations: Articulation and Compensation in the Robotic operating room

Drawing on ethnographic fieldwork in two different teaching hospitals that deployed the da Vinci surgical robot, this paper traces how the introduction of robotics reconfigures the sensory environment of surgery and how surgeons and their teams recalibrate their work in response. We illustrate how sensory (re)articulations and compensations allow the surgeon and surgical teams to adapt to a more-than-human sensorium and conclude by advocating new forms of sensory-aware design capable of enhancing and supporting embodied sensory conditions both individually and across teams.

Q: How can craft and design worlds evolve through access to new digital processes? Findings from fine art furniture production.

Digital Entanglements: Craft, Computation and Collaboration in Fine Art Furniture Production

Drawing on studio visits and interviews with fine art furniture maker Wendell Castle and his team, we investigate one studio's experience with integrating digital fabrication tools into their studio practice, and its implications for the collective organization of work and creativity. We chart new forms of creative practice and material flow that emerge from this encounter, and show how remediations of craft in the Castle studio may pose questions and opportunities for wider CSCW concerns around craft, creativity, and design.
